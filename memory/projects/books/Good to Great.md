# Good to Great

**By [[Jim Collins]]** | 2001 | 320 pages

#business #leadership #strategy #management #culture

![[Good to Great.png]]

## One-Liner

Eleven companies that went from average to outstanding all shared a handful of disciplined behaviors — humble leadership, rigorous focus, and slow-burning consistency, not flashy revolutions.

## Key Principles

### Level 5 Leadership

The CEOs of Collins's "great" companies were paradoxical — personally humble, professionally relentless. They credited the team for wins and took blame for losses. No rockstars, no theatrics. Think Tim Cook, not Elon Musk. The lesson: when interviewing leaders, beware the ones who can't stop talking about themselves.

### First Who, Then What

Get the right people on the bus before deciding where to drive. With A-players in the seats, the team will figure out the destination together. With B-players, no strategy will save you. Most companies do the opposite — they pick a direction and scramble to staff it. Flip that.

### Confront the Brutal Facts

Build a culture where bad news travels fast and the messenger doesn't get shot. Pair this with the [[Stockdale Paradox]] — keep faith you'll prevail in the end while accepting the most brutal facts of your present reality. The tension between those is the muscle.

### The Hedgehog Concept

The most useful idea in the book. Find the intersection of three circles: what you can be **best in the world** at, what drives your **economic engine**, and what you're **deeply passionate** about. The overlap is your hedgehog. The discipline is in the no, not the yes.

### Culture of Discipline

Disciplined people remove the need for hierarchy. Disciplined thought removes the need for bureaucracy. Disciplined action removes the need for excessive controls. Bureaucracy is the tax you pay for hiring the wrong people.

### The Flywheel Effect

Greatness isn't a single dramatic push — it's thousands of consistent shoves in the same direction until momentum carries the wheel itself. The flip side is the **Doom Loop**, where companies keep changing direction and never let momentum build.

## Actionable Takeaways

- [ ] Audit your team. Are these the people you'd hire again today? If not, that's your first move — not strategy.
- [ ] Write down your Hedgehog: what you're best at, what makes money, what you love. The overlap is your business.
- [ ] Create a "stop doing" list. As powerful as your to-do list. Kill projects that don't fit the Hedgehog.
- [ ] Hold weekly meetings where bad news is welcomed, not punished. Lead by asking questions, not delivering answers.

## What You Can Skip

- **The "company examples" deep-dives** — Half the companies he picked (Circuit City, Fannie Mae, Wells Fargo) later collapsed or imploded. The case studies aged badly.
- **The "Technology Accelerators" chapter** — Stuck in 2001. Skip it.
- **The methodology appendix** — Phil Rosenzweig's [[The Halo Effect]] already dismantled it. Read that instead if you want the critique.

## Verdict

A useful book if you read it as mental models, not scientific gospel. The Hedgehog, Level 5, and Flywheel are genuinely sticky frameworks worth knowing. But the "we proved this with data" framing is survivorship bias dressed in a suit. Take the concepts, leave the certainty.

See also: [[Built to Last]] (Collins's earlier work), [[How the Mighty Fall]] (his awkward sequel about why "great" companies later imploded), [[The Halo Effect]] (the takedown).
