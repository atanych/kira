# Continuous Discovery Habits
**[[Teresa Torres]]** · 2021

![[Continuous Discovery Habits.png]]

## One-Liner
Stop doing "research projects." Talk to customers every week, map opportunities to outcomes, and test your riskiest assumptions before you build anything.

## Key Principles

### 1. Discovery is a habit, not a project
Weekly customer touchpoints beat quarterly research sprints. Cadence over perfection — even 1-2 interviews a week, every week, beats giant batches four times a year.

### 2. Outcomes over outputs
Measure success by customer behavior changed, not features shipped. "Increase activation 10%" not "ship onboarding v2." Gives the team freedom to find the actual best solution.

### 3. Opportunity Solution Tree (OST)
The flagship framework. Outcome at the top → customer opportunities (unmet needs/pains from real interviews) → candidate solutions → assumption tests. It's a thinking tool that forces honesty about what you know vs. assume. See also: [[Lean Startup]], [[Jobs to be Done]].

### 4. Interview for stories, not opinions
"Tell me about the last time you…" beats "Would you use…" every time. People are terrible at predicting their own future behavior. Mine for actual past behavior, not aspirations.

### 5. Map assumptions before building
Five categories: desirability, viability, feasibility, usability, ethical. Identify the riskiest assumptions, then design the smallest test that could *falsify* them. Not prove right — prove wrong.

### 6. Compare solutions, don't evaluate in isolation
Your brain anchors on the first idea. Always generate 3+ solutions per opportunity before debating which to build. Comparison surfaces tradeoffs single-solution thinking hides.

### 7. The product trio
PM + designer + engineer make discovery decisions together. No more specs over the wall. Aspirational for solo/small teams, but the principle holds — discovery is a team sport.

## Actionable Takeaways
- [ ] Block a recurring weekly slot for customer interviews
- [ ] Build an OST for current outcome — sketch on paper, update weekly
- [ ] Rewrite every "would you / do you want" question into "tell me about the last time you…"
- [ ] Before scoping any solution, write down the 3 riskiest assumptions and test the riskiest this week
- [ ] For every opportunity, brainstorm at least 3 solutions before debating which to build

## What You Can Skip
- **Trio chapters** — if solo or small team, skim org-design parts
- **Middle repetition** — chapters 6-8 restate OST in different ways; once you get it, move on
- **Recruiting deep-dive** — useful, not memorable; bookmark for when you need it

## Verdict
Genuinely one of the best product books of the last 5 years. The OST alone is worth the price — it's a thinking tool, not just a framework. Torres is opinionated without being preachy, and every chapter ends with something you can do Monday morning. If you ship product and aren't doing weekly customer touchpoints, this book is going to roast you (gently). Mandatory for PMs, useful for any builder.
