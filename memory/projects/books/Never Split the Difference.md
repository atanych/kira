# Never Split the Difference

**Author:** [[Chris Voss]] (with [[Tahl Raz]])
**Year:** 2016 · **Pages:** 288
**Tags:** #negotiation #sales #psychology #communication

## One-Liner
Negotiation isn't logic or splitting the difference — it's tactical empathy, because humans are irrational and feelings drive every decision.

## Key Principles

### 1. Tactical Empathy
Understand the other side's feelings *and* show you understand them. Not agreement — acknowledgement. The whole book is built on this one idea: you don't beat the other side, you tune into them until they hand you the deal.

### 2. Mirroring
Repeat the last 1-3 words of what they just said, with an upward inflection, like a question. They'll keep talking and fill in the gaps. Borderline magical — works even when they know you're doing it.

### 3. Labeling
Name the emotion out loud. "It sounds like…" / "It seems like…" / "It looks like…" Never "I feel" — that makes it about you. Labels defuse negatives (anger, fear) by making them visible, and reinforce positives.

### 4. "That's Right" — not "You're Right"
"You're right" is the phrase people use to shut you up and end the conversation. "That's right" is what they say when they actually feel understood. That's the unlock. Summarize their position until they say it.

### 5. Calibrated "How" and "What" Questions
"How am I supposed to do that?" quietly forces them to solve your problem for you. "What about this works for you?" reveals priorities. Never ask "why" — it sounds accusatory in every language.

### 6. "No" Is the Start, Not the End
People feel safe and in control when they say no. "Is now a bad time to talk?" gets more real answers than "Do you have a minute?" Trigger their no, earn their engagement.

### 7. The Accusation Audit
Before you make a hard ask, list every bad thing they might think about you — out loud. "You're probably going to think this is another pitch…" Defangs objections before they fire.

### 8. Hunt the Black Swan
Assume 3 pieces of info you don't have would completely change the negotiation. Stay curious, listen long, and surface them. Most deals die because someone didn't dig for the unknown.

## Actionable Takeaways
- [ ] Open every cold call with "Is now a bad time to talk?" — flips the script, earns a real answer.
- [ ] Mirror the last 3 words of anything you don't understand. Then shut up. Let them fill the silence.
- [ ] Label emotions out loud: "It seems like you've been burned by this before…" Pause.
- [ ] Replace every "why" with "how" or "what." "Why did you do that?" → "What led to that decision?"
- [ ] Run an Accusation Audit before every tough ask. Voice their worst objections first.
- [ ] In every discovery call, hunt for the "that's right" moment. Summarize their pain until they say it.

## Verdict
The actual playbook for anyone who talks to humans for money. Voss is the anti-Harvard: where [[Getting to Yes]] claims negotiation is rational problem-solving, Voss says that's fiction and feelings run everything. He's right. Three chapters — mirroring, labeling, calibrated questions — are worth the whole book. Re-read them before every high-stakes call.
