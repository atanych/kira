## One-Liner
Skip the $200k MBA. Every business does the same five things — create value, market it, sell it, deliver it, manage money — and [[Josh Kaufman]] compresses the fundamentals into one reference manual.

## Key Principles

### 1. Five Parts of Every Business
Value Creation, Marketing, Sales, Value Delivery, Finance. Miss one and it's not a business, it's a hobby. Use it as a checklist when evaluating any venture — yours or someone else's.

### 2. The Iron Law of the Market
A market that wants what you're selling beats a great product nobody asked for. "No market" kills more startups than bad code. Validate demand *before* you build.

### 3. Core Human Drives
Every purchase hits one of five — Acquire, Bond, Learn, Defend, Feel. Hit two and you've got a winner. Pitch through the drives, not the features.

### 4. 12 Forms of Value
Product, service, shared resource, subscription, resale, lease, agency, audience aggregation, loan, option, insurance, capital. Pick one. Stop trying to be all twelve.

### 5. Four Methods to Grow Revenue
More customers, bigger transactions, more frequent transactions, raise prices. That's the entire list. If your "growth strategy" doesn't map to one of those, it's a vibe, not a strategy.

### 6. Sufficiency Over Perfection
"Good enough" shipped beats "perfect" stuck in draft. Define the minimum, ship, iterate.

### 7. Hook, Story, Offer
Get attention, build a narrative, make a clear ask. Most marketing skips the story and dies.

### 8. Sell the End Result
Customers don't buy drills, they buy holes. Kill feature-list copy, name the outcome.

## Actionable Takeaways
- [ ] Audit your current business/project against the 5 parts. Fix the weakest one first.
- [ ] Map your product to Core Human Drives. Less than two? Probably not worth it.
- [ ] Map your revenue plan to the 4 growth levers. If you can't, you don't have a plan.
- [ ] Rewrite your landing page in "end result" language — kill bullets that don't tie to outcomes.
- [ ] Use this book as an index. Skim once, return to chapters when problems hit in real life.

## Verdict
It's a business-fundamentals dictionary, not a manifesto. None of the ideas are original — Kaufman synthesizes from 100 better books — but the compression *is* the value. Treat it like a reference manual: skim once, come back to the right chapter when you hit a real problem.

If you've never absorbed business 101, this is the fastest legal path to "fluent." If you've already read [[Peter Drucker]], [[Robert Cialdini]], [[Eric Ries]], and [[Daniel Kahneman]] — heavy déjà vu. Worth owning. Not worth a cover-to-cover deep read.
