# Rich Dad Poor Dad

**Author:** [[Robert Kiyosaki]]
**Year:** 1997 · **Pages:** 336
**Tags:** #finance #mindset #investing #passive-income

## One-Liner

Stop trading time for money. Learn the difference between assets and liabilities, buy assets that generate income, and make money work for you instead of the other way around.

## Key Lessons

### 1. Assets vs. Liabilities

The foundation of the entire book. An asset puts money in your pocket. A liability takes money out. Your house? Liability — mortgage, taxes, maintenance, insurance. A rental property that cash-flows? Asset.

Most people spend their lives buying liabilities (cars, gadgets, bigger houses) thinking they're building wealth, when they're actually building expenses. The rich buy assets first and let those assets pay for the luxuries.

This reframe alone is worth more than most finance courses.

### 2. Make Money Work For You

Poor Dad's advice: get good grades, get a stable job, work hard, save. Rich Dad's advice: learn how money works and make it work for you.

The shift from active income (trading time for paycheck) to passive income (owning things that generate money without your direct involvement). Investments, businesses, intellectual property, real estate. Sounds obvious in 2026, but in 1997 this was radical for most people.

### 3. Pay Yourself First

Before bills, before rent, before anyone — set aside money for investing. Even 10%. This creates positive pressure: when bills are still due, you're forced to find creative ways to cover the gap. Builds financial discipline.

Modern personal finance universally agrees: automate savings, invest before you spend.

### 4. Financial Education > Formal Education

Schools teach you to be a good employee but never teach you about money, taxes, investing, or how businesses work. Most people graduate knowing calculus but not how compound interest works.

Financial literacy is a skill you have to teach yourself. Read books, study tax law, understand accounting basics. This is the education that actually determines your financial outcome.

See also: [[Think and Grow Rich]] (specialized knowledge principle)

### 5. Work to Learn, Not to Earn

Choose jobs based on what skills they teach, not what they pay. Rich Dad specifically recommended learning sales and marketing — those skills transfer to everything.

Even if you're a developer or engineer, knowing how to sell, communicate value, and handle rejection is a force multiplier.

See also: [[Atomic Habits]] (skill stacking), [[Buy Back Your Time]] (high-value activities)

### 6. Build Systems, Not Jobs

The [[Cashflow Quadrant]]: left side (employees, self-employed) trade time for money linearly. Right side (business owners, investors) build systems that generate money exponentially. The goal is to move from left to right.

## Actionable Takeaways

- [ ] List everything you own — classify each as asset (generates income) or liability (costs money). Be honest
- [ ] Set up automatic transfers — pay yourself first, even 10%. Invest before you spend
- [ ] Spend 30 min/day on financial literacy — taxes, investing, accounting basics
- [ ] Next career move: optimize for skills learned, not salary offered. Sales and marketing are the highest-leverage skills

## What You Can Skip

- **"Just buy real estate" advice** — Kiyosaki makes it sound easy and risk-free. It's not. Requires capital, knowledge, and market timing. Don't YOLO into property because a book told you to
- **Anti-education rhetoric** — Formal education isn't useless, it's incomplete. "Drop out and start a business" is survivorship bias
- **Tax avoidance schemes** — The corporation-to-avoid-taxes stuff is oversimplified to the point of being misleading. Talk to an actual accountant

## Verdict

The mindset shift is the real value — assets vs. liabilities, passive income, financial self-education. That framework genuinely changes how you think about money. But the book is light on specifics and heavy on "trust me bro" energy. Think of it as the red pill, not the instruction manual.
