# The Mom Test

**Author:** [[Rob Fitzpatrick]]
**Year:** 2013 · **Pages:** 130
**Tags:** #startup #customer-dev #sales #research

## One-Liner
Customers lie to be nice — stop asking if your idea is good and start asking about their life, so the truth leaks out even when they're trying to flatter you.

## Key Principles

### 1. The Mom Test
Frame questions so well that even your mom couldn't lie. If the question requires opinions about your idea, you've already failed.

### 2. Talk about their life, not your idea
The moment your idea hits the table, everyone shifts to polite mode. Keep the spotlight on what they do, what they tried, what they hate.

### 3. Past specifics beat future hypotheticals
"When did you last do X?" is gold. "Would you use a product that…?" is trash. People are terrible at predicting, great at reporting.

### 4. Three bad data types: compliments, fluff, ideas
Praise means nothing. Hypotheticals are noise. Customer feature requests aren't orders — dig into the problem underneath.

### 5. Commitment & advancement
Real signals: time (next meeting booked), reputation (intros made), money (prepayment, LOI). No commitment = not a customer.

### 6. Know your 3 big questions
Before every call, write down the 3 things you need to learn. Don't know what you're hunting? You won't catch it.

### 7. Keep it casual
Formal interviews trigger PR mode. Coffee chats and corridor convos get real answers.

### 8. Write it down immediately
Verbatim quotes, not paraphrases. Memory bends toward what you wanted to hear. Review notes weekly for patterns.

## Actionable Takeaways
- [ ] Before every customer call: write the 3 big questions. No 3 questions, no call.
- [ ] Replace every "would you…" with "when was the last time you…"
- [ ] Never pitch your idea in an interview. Deflect: "I'll tell you after — right now I want to understand your setup."
- [ ] End every meeting with a commitment ask (next meeting, intro, or prepayment).
- [ ] Take verbatim notes. Re-read weekly to spot repeating pain points.
