# Think and Grow Rich

**Author:** [[Napoleon Hill]]
**Year:** 1937 · **Pages:** 238
**Tags:** #mindset #success #wealth

## One-Liner

Success starts in your head — get obsessively clear on what you want, believe you'll get it, build a plan, surround yourself with sharp people, and never quit.

## Key Principles

### 1. Burning Desire

Not a wish, not "it'd be nice." A specific, obsessive goal with a deadline and a price you're willing to pay. Write it down. Read it daily.

Hill wants you to define the exact amount, the exact deadline, and what you're willing to give in return. Read it out loud every morning and night. Modern goal-setting research backs this — specific written goals with deadlines massively outperform vague intentions.

### 2. Faith + Autosuggestion

Reprogram your brain by repeating your goal until your subconscious buys in. Sounds woo-woo, but it's what modern psychology calls "priming" and "self-efficacy."

When you repeatedly visualize and affirm a goal, your brain starts filtering for opportunities that align with it. You don't magically attract success — you stop being blind to it. Key nuance Hill misses: visualization works best paired with action. Imagining the *process* drives behavior more than imagining the *outcome*.

### 3. Specialized Knowledge

General knowledge is useless for making money. Go deep in your lane, not shallow in everything. More importantly — know WHO knows things, not everything yourself.

Hill argues you don't need to know it all. You need to know where to find information and who to ask. In 1937 that meant libraries and mentors. Today it's the same plus the internet. The principle holds: depth beats breadth.

### 4. Organized Planning

Dreams without a plan are just fantasies. Build a concrete step-by-step plan and start executing immediately — not when conditions are perfect.

The part most people miss: Hill says your first plan will probably fail. And your second one might too. That's not the problem. The problem is when people *stop making new plans*. Direct quote: "Temporary defeat is not permanent failure." This is basically [[Lean Startup]] methodology, seventy years early.

### 5. Mastermind Group

The highest-leverage idea in the entire book. Two or more people working together in harmony toward a definite purpose create a force greater than the sum of its parts.

Every massively successful person Hill studied had a mastermind group. Carnegie had his advisors. Ford had Edison and Firestone. Nobody succeeds alone. If you take one thing from this book — build a mastermind group of 3-5 people who challenge and complement your thinking.

See also: [[Atomic Habits]] (environment design), [[Buy Back Your Time]] (delegation)

### 6. Persistence

The #1 reason for failure is simply giving up too early. Not lack of talent, not bad luck — just quitting.

Hill gives the famous example of a gold miner who quit digging just three feet from a massive gold vein. Someone else bought his equipment, dug three more feet, and struck it rich. Persistence isn't grinding mindlessly — it's having such clear desire and such a solid plan that setbacks feel like speed bumps, not roadblocks.

## Actionable Takeaways

- [ ] Write down your #1 goal with a specific deadline and exact metric. Read it out loud every morning and night
- [ ] Build a mastermind group — 3-5 people you meet regularly who challenge your thinking
- [ ] When your plan fails, don't stop — rewrite the plan. Failure = feedback, not a verdict
- [ ] Invest 30 min/day in specialized knowledge in your field. Depth beats breadth

## What You Can Skip

- **Sex Transmutation chapter** — Hill argues sexual energy can be redirected into creative genius. Product of its time
- **The Sixth Sense / "Infinite Intelligence"** — Mystical hand-waving. The useful part (trust your gut after building expertise) doesn't need a whole chapter
- **"Vibrations" and thought transmission** — Pseudoscience. Your thoughts don't literally broadcast to the universe

## Verdict

The OG of self-help. The core ideas — clarity of purpose, persistence, mastermind groups — are legit and hold up 90 years later. But about 40% of the book is filler, repetition, and pseudoscience. The actionable stuff could fit in a blog post.
